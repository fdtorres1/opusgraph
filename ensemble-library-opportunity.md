## Executive Summary

This document tracks the opportunity for a cloud-based SaaS platform serving the ensemble music library management market. The product would replace spreadsheets and outdated tools used by orchestras, choirs, bands, and other performing ensembles to catalog sheet music, track parts and copies, manage performance history, and plan seasons.

The opportunity was identified through structured market validation research in March 2026.

| Dimension | Assessment |

**Key notes on market sizing:**

- 77% of U.S. orchestras have annual budgets under $300,000. These are the organizations most likely stuck on spreadsheets and most price-sensitive.
- Church choirs are the largest segment by raw count but are declining (from 50%+ of congregations in 1998 to ~40% by 2018-19). Still represents ~70,000+ active programs.
- 54 million Americans (43M adults + 11M children) sing in choirs. Participation has grown from 14% to 17% since 2008.
- MOLA (Major Orchestra Librarians' Association) represents 340+ professional organizations with 560+ librarians. These are potential evangelists but unlikely early customers (they use OPAS and custom solutions).
- International market exists but is harder to size. UK alone has ~40,000 choral groups.

### Internationalization: Honest Assessment

**What AI localization actually gets you:** UI strings, help text, email templates, marketing copy in target languages. This is real and removes the traditional cost barrier to going multi-language.

**What AI localization does NOT get you:** Distribution, trust, community presence, or local market knowledge. In the U.S. you have MSO, MOLA contacts, the Texas music community. In Germany you're cold-calling into 60K+ Gesangvereine with zero network.

**Key caveats by market:**

- **Germany (biggest prize, hardest):** German buyers expect native-quality German copy, GDPR compliance from day one, German payment methods (SEPA), and German-language support. The 60K choir number is massive but overwhelmingly volunteer-run with tiny budgets. Conversion rates will be well below U.S. without local champions.
- **UK (easiest expansion):** English-speaking, similar ensemble culture, no localization beyond currency. 40K choirs with growing participation. Obvious first international market.
- **Japan (high density, high barrier):** 1,600 orchestras is impressive density, but Japanese localization, cultural UX expectations, and zero distribution make this later-stage at best.
- **Nordics (high participation, tiny absolute numbers):** Sweden's 17% choir participation rate is extraordinary, but absolute numbers are small. Nice-to-have, not a priority.
- **South Korea (growing, but immature):** Amateur scene is booming but still young. Interesting future market, not a launch target.
- **The choir segment changes the product.** German Gesangvereine and UK community choirs have different workflows than U.S. community orchestras. Choirs prioritize voicing, liturgical calendar, copies-per-member. Orchestras care about instrumentation, rental parts, bowings. Chasing the international choir market pulls the product in a different direction than MSO dogfooding.
- **Price sensitivity gets worse internationally.** A quarter of Australian community choirs operate on annual budgets under $1,000. German volunteer choirs are similar. A generous free tier becomes even more critical outside the U.S.

**Recommended expansion sequence:**

1. **U.S. community orchestras** — dogfood at MSO, validate PMF
2. **U.S. community choruses** — adjacent segment, same language, test choir-specific features
3. **UK** — English-speaking, no localization cost, strong choir growth
4. **Germany** — biggest international prize, requires real localization investment and at least one local champion
5. **Everything else** — opportunistic, follow inbound demand

**Bottom line:** Internationalization moves the theoretical ceiling from ~$40-96K to ~$150-460K ARR. That's material — it shifts from "side project" to "could be a real small business." But the realistic path is still U.S. first, prove PMF, then expand. AI localization removes the cost barrier to multi-language UI; it doesn't remove the distribution barrier.

### Direct Competitors

| Product | What It Does | Strengths | Weaknesses |
|---------|-------------|-----------|------------|
| **Archive440** | Cloud SaaS for sheet music cataloging, performance tracking, budgeting. $9-14/mo. | Purpose-built for ensembles. Import from spreadsheets. Multi-user roles. PDF storage. | Typo-riddled marketing. Small team. Limited discoverability. No mobile app evident. No community features. |
| **MusicLibrarian.net** | Web-based catalog for bands, orchestras, choirs. | Exists. Has some user base. | Looks like a 2005 side project. "Contact the Webmaster." No visible development. |
| **OPAS** | Computerized database for professional orchestra librarians. | Used by major orchestras. Deep repertoire data. | Not accessible to community ensembles. Not modern SaaS. Not self-serve. |
| **OLTK** | Desktop tool for PDF part preparation: batch rename, crop, rotate, print. | Loved by professional librarians. Built by former librarian. Solves real pain. | Adjacent, not competitive. Does file prep, not cataloging or inventory. |
| **Google Sheets / Excel** | The actual incumbent. Used by the vast majority of ensembles. | Free. Familiar. Flexible. "Good enough." | No multi-faceted search. No performance history. No copy tracking. Breaks at scale. Data loss risk. |

### Competitive Assessment

Archive440 is the only real SaaS competitor and their execution leaves clear room for improvement. Their pricing page contains typos, their marketing is minimal, and their feature set — while adequate — doesn't appear to have evolved significantly. There is no evidence of a mobile app, API, or integration with any external tools.

The true competitor is inertia. Most ensembles are on spreadsheets and have been for years. The challenge is not beating Archive440; it's convincing organizations to move off spreadsheets at all.

## Validated Pain Points

The following pain points were identified through forum posts, blog articles, and community discussions from choir directors, church musicians, orchestra librarians, and band directors. Sources include MusicaSacra forums, Choir Director Corner, MOLA resources, and various music education communities.

### 1. Spreadsheets Are Universal and Universally Inadequate

- Every guide, blog post, and forum thread defaults to recommending Excel or Google Sheets as the cataloging solution.
- One church with 40-50 choir members and 10+ shelving units described their Excel file as a "temporary solution" that became permanent after 5+ years.
- Choir Director Corner reports getting questions "pretty regularly" from directors struggling with library organization, calling it "pretty overwhelming."
- Forum users describe physically searching through music as "tedious and a HUGE pain."

### 2. Physical Organization Is Chaotic

- A choir librarian described inheriting "mildew in the corner, shelves scattered everywhere, music stacked in boxes that also hold T-shirts and polos from choirs of eons ago."
- Churches ordering 65 copies of every anthem, numbering each one 1-65, tracking which member has which copy — all by hand.
- Seasonal music (Christmas, Easter, Lent) creates recurring organizational challenges that spreadsheets handle poorly.

### 3. Multi-Faceted Search Is a Core Unmet Need

- Directors want to search simultaneously by voicing, composer, season, instrumentation, number of copies, and accompaniment type.
- One forum poster wished for a catalog organized by Biblical reference to match the Lectionary — a feature no spreadsheet easily provides.
- Knowing "what do we have for SATB with organ that works for Advent and has 50+ copies" requires multiple manual filters.

### 4. Performance History Tracking Is Desired but Nonexistent

- Multiple sources mention wanting to know "when was this last performed" and "how many times have we used this piece."
- Season planning requires knowing what's been done recently to avoid repetition — currently tracked in memory or ad-hoc notes.
- One community chorus's concert program came together late and contained inaccuracies that upset donors — directly tied to poor library tracking.

### 5. Copy/Part Tracking Is Manual and Error-Prone

- Rental sets (who checked out what, condition, missing parts) are tracked on paper or not at all for smaller organizations.
- Churches process 40-60 binders weekly, swapping music in/out by hand. Missing copies are discovered only when someone reports it.
- No existing lightweight tool handles "we own 52 copies of this piece, 3 are missing, last performed Easter 2024."

## Feature Priorities (Preliminary)

Based on validated pain points. This is a hypothesis to be tested through dogfooding and early user conversations, not a committed roadmap.

### MVP (Build for MSO)

| Feature | Priority | Rationale |
|---------|----------|-----------|
| Catalog: title, composer, arranger, publisher, instrumentation/voicing | **P0** | Core value. Replaces spreadsheet. |
| Copy/part count tracking per piece | **P0** | Most-cited missing feature from spreadsheets. |
| Multi-field search and filtering | **P0** | The #1 reason spreadsheets fail at scale. |
| Performance history (date, event, pieces used) | **P0** | Season planning depends on this. Highly requested. |
| CSV/spreadsheet import | **P0** | Migration path from current tools. Archive440 offers this. |
| Category/tag system (season, genre, difficulty) | P1 | Important for churches (liturgical season) and schools (difficulty level). |
| PDF upload and attachment to catalog entries | P1 | Nice to have. Archive440 offers this at higher tiers. |
| Multi-user access with roles | P1 | Needed for team use but not for solo dogfooding. |

### Future Differentiators (Post-MVP)

- **Rental set tracking:** checkout/return workflow, condition notes, missing part alerts. Unique to publishers and larger libraries.
- **Concert program generator:** pull from catalog to auto-generate program notes and printed programs.
- **Budget tracking per performance:** cost of purchased music, rental fees, licensing.
- **IMSLP/public domain integration:** link catalog entries to freely available scores.
- **Mobile companion (iOS):** quick lookup during rehearsals without a laptop.
- **Wish list with community voting:** board/committee members rate potential purchases.

## Risks and Open Questions

### Risks

| Risk | Severity | Mitigation |
|------|----------|------------|
| Market too small to sustain meaningful revenue | **HIGH** | Build lean. Keep as complement to other business lines, not standalone bet. |
| Spreadsheet inertia: orgs won't switch | **HIGH** | Target organizations that have already tried and failed with spreadsheets. Lead with import. |
| Budget-constrained buyers: can't pay $10-15/mo | MEDIUM | Generous free tier. Price at $9-14/mo (Archive440 range). Offer annual discount. |
| Archive440 improves and closes the gap | LOW | They've shown minimal evolution. Execute on UX and niche features. |
| Scope creep: trying to serve all segments at once | MEDIUM | Start with community orchestras. Expand only after PMF. |

### Open Questions

1. Would MSO actually use this daily, or would it become shelfware? Dogfooding is the first real test.
2. Can 5 other community ensembles be convinced to pay before building beyond MVP?
3. Who is the buyer? The music director, the volunteer librarian, the board treasurer, or the admin? Different buyers have different pain points and budget authority.
4. Is the choir/church segment worth pursuing, or is it too price-sensitive and too different from orchestras in workflow?
5. What does Archive440's actual user base look like? Are they growing, stagnant, or declining?
6. How much overlap is there between this and the parts rental tracking idea (Resonance)? Could they be the same product?
